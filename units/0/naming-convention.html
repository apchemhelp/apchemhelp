Naming Conventions

Now that we know what an ion and element are, let’s talk about what happens after you make a compound. Specifically, naming a compound.

Take a look at this chart, which pretty much summarizes everything about the naming conventions.

Let’s start with ionic compounds, which consist of metals and nonmetals. The naming is relatively easy. It’s just the name of the metal and nonmetal with an -ide ending.

Now comes the nonmetals. What happens if you combine two nonmetals? Simple, you will use the greek numeral to represent the number of atoms for a particular element. For instance, PCl5 will be called Phosphorus pentachloride and _________. Note that we don’t add mono (if applicable) in front of the first element.

Last but not least, we have the polyatomic ion. What’s that you ask? It’s typically when two or more ions are covalently bonded (which we will explain further in Atomic Theory) to form new ions. Due to the charge present in ions, the naming conventions differ from nonmetals. Let’s first take a look at the most common polyatomic ions with their chemical formulas, charges and names.
