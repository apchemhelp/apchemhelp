Atomic Structure

The Nucleus

The nucleus of an atom contains the two largest subatomic particles: protons and neutrons. Protons are a type of subatomic particle that have a positive charge. They provide the nucleus with a positive charge and help keep the electrons attracted to the nucleus so that they don't fly away. Protons are in every atom. Then there are neutrons which have a neutral charge. They are present in every atom except for the Hydrogen-1(for more info on naming conventions go to Unit 0). The purpose of a neutron is to provide stability in the nucleus and to prevent protons from repelling each other. These subatomic particles weigh really small. Take protons for example, their weight is 1.67 × 10–27 kg.

Electrons

Electrons are the smallest out of the three subatomic particles and they have a negative charge. Instead of being in the nucleus like the rest of the subatomic particles, it orbits around the nucleus. You might be asking, why doesn't the electron get flung out? The reason for this is that it is attracted to the positive charge of the nucleus. You may have heard the term that opposites attract and it is that very property that holds the atom together.
